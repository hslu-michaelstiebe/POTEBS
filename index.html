Pragmatism, not ideology:
what really drives e-bike sharing use?

POTEBS investigates e-bike sharing in the Basel metropolitan area by combining large-scale operational records with linked survey data. The latest results suggest that intensive use is primarily driven by demonstrated weather resilience, multimodal integration, and perceived functional utility.

Electric bike sharing appears useful in a real but bounded way.

Rather than being mainly an expression of environmental ideology, intensive EBSS use seems to emerge where shared e-bikes solve concrete mobility problems better than available alternatives.

Key findings

What the newest results suggest

This version shifts the website more clearly from project description to core empirical messages.

Usage is highly concentrated

The top 20% of users generate 80.3% of all trips. A relatively small group of power users is therefore central to system viability.

Power users are pragmatists

High-intensity use is associated with weather resilience, multimodal complementarity, and perceived functional superiority over public transport.

Environmental identity is not the key driver

In the combined model, pro-environmental self-identity does not predict intensive use positively and is negatively associated with power-user status.

EBSS mainly substitutes public transport

In Basel’s transit-rich setting, public transport is the most frequently substituted mode, while car substitution remains more limited.

System design matters

Dock-based and free-floating services attract different pragmatic usage profiles while operating in the same broader region.

Policy should stay realistic

Shared e-bikes appear most useful as one component within a multimodal system rather than as a standalone solution.

Natural experiment

Two system designs in one metropolitan area

Basel offers a rare comparison between free-floating S-Pedelec sharing and a dock-based e-bike sharing system operating concurrently in the same broader context.

Pick-e-Bike

Free-floating

Broader agglomeration coverage, point-to-point flexibility, and S-Pedelec operation make this system well suited for flexible mobility chains.

  • Vehicle type: S-Pedelec
  • Top speed: up to 45 km/h, with regulated 25 km/h speed-setting option
  • Spatial pattern: broader service area across Basel and surrounding municipalities
  • Emerging archetype: flexibility maximizers

PubliBike / Velospot

Dock-based

A station-based urban system with stronger routine orientation and commuter logic in the Basel core and selected nearby municipalities.

  • Vehicle type: conventional e-bike
  • Top speed: 25 km/h
  • Spatial pattern: station network concentrated in the urban core
  • Emerging archetype: routine optimizers

39.2% of linked users. Weekly to bi-weekly use. EBSS is already part of the mobility toolkit, but not yet deeply embedded into daily routines.

High intensity

12.71 RPM

13.1% of linked users. Usage every two to three days. These power users are central to understanding economic viability and sustained demand.

Archetypes

Two distinct power-user profiles

The latest results point to two different high-intensity archetypes shaped by system design rather than by a fundamentally different ideology.

Routine optimizers

The dock-based power-user profile appears more commuter-oriented, more predictable, and more strongly tied to routine optimization and corridor-based travel.

Flexibility maximizers

The free-floating power-user profile appears more point-to-point oriented, more multimodal, and more focused on flexible adaptation within daily mobility chains.

Policy implications

What this means for sustainable mobility policy

Focus on reliability

Availability, operational consistency, and affordability appear central if occasional users are to become regular riders.

Strengthen PT integration

The strongest user logic is multimodal. Integration with public transport apps, tickets, and hubs should therefore be a priority.

Communicate utility, not only sustainability

Time savings, flexibility, and convenience may resonate more strongly with likely power users than purely environmental framing.

Use realistic success metrics

EBSS should be assessed not only by direct car substitution, but also by network effects, fallback value, and multimodal complementarity.

Keep a portfolio view

Dock-based and free-floating systems may serve complementary roles. Urban routines and flexible peri-urban travel are not the same use case.

Treat EBSS as one piece of the puzzle

The project does not support simplistic miracle narratives. Shared e-bikes seem most valuable when embedded in a broader transport system.
