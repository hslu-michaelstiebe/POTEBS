POTEBS

Investigating the Potential of E-Bike-Sharing Systems for Sustainable Mobility in Different Spatial Types

POTEBS investigates e-bike sharing in the Basel metropolitan area by combining large-scale operational records with linked survey data. The current results suggest that intensive use is primarily driven by demonstrated weather resilience, multimodal integration, and perceived functional utility.

Electric bike sharing appears useful in a real but bounded way.

Rather than being mainly an expression of environmental ideology, intensive EBSS use seems to emerge where shared e-bikes solve concrete mobility problems better than available alternatives.

Key findings

What the current results suggest

This version shifts the website more clearly from project description to core empirical messages and uses neutral wording throughout.

Usage is highly concentrated

The top 20% of users generate 80.3% of all trips. A relatively small group of power users is therefore central to system viability.

Power users are pragmatists

High-intensity use is associated with weather resilience, multimodal complementarity, and perceived functional superiority over public transport.

Environmental identity is not the key driver

In the combined model, pro-environmental self-identity does not positively predict intensive use and is negatively associated with power-user status.

EBSS mainly substitutes public transport

In Basel’s transit-rich setting, public transport is the most frequently substituted mode, while car substitution remains more limited.

System design matters

Dock-based and free-floating services attract different pragmatic usage profiles while operating in the same broader region.

Policy should remain realistic

Shared e-bikes appear most useful as one component within a multimodal system rather than as a standalone solution.

Natural experiment

Two system designs in one metropolitan area

Basel offers a rare comparison between free-floating S-Pedelec sharing and a dock-based e-bike-sharing system operating concurrently in the same broader context.

Pick-e-Bike

Free-floating

Broader agglomeration coverage, point-to-point flexibility, and S-Pedelec operation make this system well suited to flexible mobility chains.

  • Vehicle type: S-Pedelec
  • Top speed: up to 45 km/h, with regulated 25 km/h speed-setting option
  • Area basis on this page: municipality polygons from the uploaded service-area map
  • Typical high-use profile: flexibility maximizers

PubliBike / Velospot

Dock-based

A station-based urban system with stronger routine orientation and commuter logic in the Basel core and selected nearby municipalities.

  • Vehicle type: conventional e-bike
  • Top speed: 25 km/h
  • Area basis on this page: uploaded station coordinates
  • Typical high-use profile: routine optimizers
Methodology

How the analysis was constructed

For documentation purposes, the page now includes a more explicit methodological description. This can later be extended into a more formal process chart or a dedicated methods subpage.

1. Data integration

Operational trip data from both providers were cleaned, harmonized, and prepared for comparative analysis.

2. Survey fieldwork

User survey responses were collected, quality-checked, and reduced to a retained analytical sample.

3. Deterministic linkage

Consenting survey users were linked to provider-side operational records via provider-specific user identifiers.

4. Behavioral modeling

Usage intensity, attitudes, weather exposure, and multimodal integration were analyzed using segmentation and explanatory models.

5. Interpretation

Combined evidence from revealed and stated behavior was used to interpret user segments, usage concentration, system-design effects, and policy relevance.

Analytical elements referred to on this website include usage concentration analysis, Rentals per Month segmentation, linked behavioral interpretation, comparative system-design assessment, and the spatial comparison of a dock-based and a free-floating system in the same metropolitan region.

User intensity

Three usage intensity segments

Gaussian Mixture Modeling suggests a three-segment structure based on Rentals per Month. The combined descriptions below summarize the broad behavioral logic of each segment.

Low intensity

0.20 RPM

47.7% of linked users. Roughly one rental every five months. This segment includes many dormant, occasional, or exploratory users.

Medium intensity

1.60 RPM

39.2% of linked users. Weekly to bi-weekly use. EBSS is already part of the mobility toolkit, but not yet deeply embedded in everyday routines.

High intensity

12.71 RPM

13.1% of linked users. Usage every two to three days. These users are central to understanding economic viability and sustained demand.

Segment interpretation

Descriptive reading of the segments

Combined: low intensity

This segment appears to use EBSS as an occasional fallback, for experimentation, or for situation-specific convenience. EBSS is available in the repertoire, but it does not structure everyday mobility.

Combined: medium intensity

This segment appears to treat EBSS as a practical multimodal option. Users return repeatedly, but usage has not yet reached the frequency or routinization observed among power users.

Combined: high intensity

This segment appears highly pragmatic, comparatively weather-tolerant, and strongly utility-oriented. For these users, EBSS functions as a recurring and often strategically integrated mobility tool.

Provider-specific reading

How segment logic differs by provider

Free-floating system

Low-intensity users appear to rely on the system sporadically, often when point-to-point flexibility is especially valuable. Medium-intensity users seem to integrate the service more regularly into multimodal chains. High-intensity users most strongly resemble flexibility maximizers: they appear to value directness, adaptive routing, and utility across varying trip contexts.

Dock-based system

Low-intensity users appear to use the system selectively, often where station-based access fits known corridors. Medium-intensity users seem more routine-oriented and compatible with recurring travel patterns. High-intensity users most strongly resemble routine optimizers: they appear to use the system in a more structured, commuter-like, and predictable manner.

Policy implications

What this means for sustainable mobility policy

Focus on reliability

Availability, operational consistency, and affordability appear central if occasional users are to become regular riders.

Strengthen public transport integration

The strongest user logic is multimodal. Integration with public transport apps, tickets, and hubs should therefore be a priority.

Communicate utility, not only sustainability

Time savings, flexibility, and convenience may resonate more strongly with likely power users than purely environmental framing.

Use realistic success metrics

EBSS should be assessed not only by direct car substitution, but also by network effects, fallback value, and multimodal complementarity.

Keep a portfolio view

Dock-based and free-floating systems may serve complementary roles. Urban routines and flexible peri-urban travel are not the same use case.

Treat EBSS as one piece of the puzzle

The project does not support simplistic miracle narratives. Shared e-bikes seem most valuable when embedded in a broader transport system.
