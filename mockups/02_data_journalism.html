Who actually uses shared e-bikes — and who just signs up and disappears?

Linking 1,844 survey responses to 1.4 million trip records across two concurrent sharing systems in the Basel metropolitan area reveals a pattern that challenges the dominant narrative.

Michael Stiebe  HSLU Luzern With Uni Basel, PubliBike, Pick-e-Bike Updated April 2026

In debates about micromobility and the energy transition, shared e-bikes are frequently framed as a tool for environmentally motivated users. The POTEBS project finds something else entirely.

Across the two systems studied in Basel — the station-based PubliBike network and the free-floating Pick-e-Bike service — usage is extraordinarily concentrated. A small minority of riders produces the bulk of the trips, while almost half of registered users complete no trip at all.

What separates power users from dormant accounts?

POTEBS links individual survey answers to each user's actual riding record — a rare pairing in the literature — and runs a multinomial logit model on 1,743 linked cases, validated by a random forest benchmark. The model doesn't find what one might expect.

Finding 01 · Against the narrative

Pro-environmental self-identity reduces power-user likelihood.

Controlling for demographics and travel context, users with strong environmentalist self-identity are less likely to be intensive EBSS users — not more. Power use is pragmatic, not ideological.

Finding 02 · Policy implication

EBSS primarily substitutes public transport, not cars.

This has direct consequences for how emissions savings are reported and how cities should frame their investment in shared micromobility.

The bounded usefulness of electric bike sharing

Read together, the findings suggest that EBSS solves a real but narrow set of problems — multimodal last-mile connections, routine optimization, flexible trip planning — and that its social impact depends on whether operators and policymakers design around that reality rather than an idealized one.

About

POTEBS is a 2023–2027 Swiss Federal Office of Energy research project investigating the potential of e-bike sharing for sustainable mobility in urban, peri-urban, and rural spatial types.
